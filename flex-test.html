What's on the GMAT?

The GMAT includes 4 sections. Quantitative and Verbal are scored between 200 and 800. Integrated Reasoning (IR) is scored on a scale of 1-8, and Analytical Writing is hand-graded on a scale of 0-6.

The quantitative section lasts 75 mins and tests:

  • Data Sufficiency: You’re given a question with 2 statements of data, and you determine whether the statements provide enough info to answer the question.
    Tip: Quickly identify important information and eliminate answer choices.

  • Problem Solving: You’re given 5 possible answer choices that test high school math skills.
    Tip: Avoid answer traps, and brush up on high school math!

The verbal section lasts 75 minutes and tests:

  • Critical Reasoning: You’re asked to break down a short argument and answer questions about it.
    Tip: These are known for tricky wording, so read carefully!
  • Sentence Correction: You’re given a sentence with a portion underlined, followed by 5 answer choices, and asked to find the best option for the sentence.
    Tip: These often test more than one grammar point!
  • Reading Comprehension: You’re given scholarly passages in the topics of business or science and asked to answer 3 or 4 questions about each passage.
    Tip: Anticipate questions as you read!

The IR section includes 4 multi-step question types that measure your ability to evaluate information presented from multiple sources in different formats.

In the analytical writing section, you’re given a brief argument and asked to write an essay that critiques its structure and persuasiveness.
Tip: Don’t try to present your own point of view; instead, offer a critique of the author’s approach.

5 Myths About GMAT Prep

1. The GMAT tests intelligence

False. The GMAT tests your ability to recognize patterns in a certain format–and this can be learned. Put time and thought into your GMAT prep, and your score should improve by Test Day.

2. You can't really study for the GMAT

Not true. Almost everyone can benefit from a focused prep plan. Students who score in the 99th percentile are students who approach the GMAT with a modicum of respect. Some GMAT questions can be rather challenging, and unless

you're scoring perfectly on all sections on your practive tests, there's always room to improve.

3. You can't prep for the essay section

Fallacy. Though the essay section may seem less concrete than the rest of the GMAT, you can always improve your writing style. Additionally, the essay can really put your test-taking endurance to the test. Taking a four-hour test in one sitting can be tiring–so practicing yhour essay will acclimate you to the phsycial demands of Test Day.

4. You can wait until the last minute

Think again. The GMAT is not a test you can cram for.

It's true that certain concepts and question types are reasonably predictable, but there are strategies you need to master in order to predict them. A good prep course can help you do just that.

5. All hyou need is the minimum score for your school.

Not really. While lots of of business schools post minimum GMAT score requirements for thier MBA programs, that doesn't mean you'll automatically be accepted if you hit that score. To become a more competitive applicant, you'll want to score well above the minimum. Plus, a great GMAT score–as opposed to a fair one–can help you land scholarships and grants.

Is Business School Right For You?

Data shows that business school graduates earn more money over the course of their careers than their BA or BS counterparts in the job market. But financial benefits aren’t the only reason to go to business school. Here are some others:

You want to network with smart people
Not only are you going to be taking classes that are taught by renowned businesspeople, but you’re also going to be working with incredibly

driven peers. A woman in your ethics class might end up being your partner at a startup; a guy in econ might introduce you to someone at your dream company; a group of study partners might end up on your research and development team.

Business schools can also hook you up with internships and summer jobs at great companies, introducing you to real-world experience.

You want to make a career change
For someone feeling bored or dissatisfied in an existing career, an advanced degree can be a great opportunity for a shift to a new industry

Consider the journalist who wants to be a financial analyst, the retail worker who wants to head up a marketing department, the musician who wants to start his own company. All can benefit immensely from going to business school.

You want to advance in your current career
If you’re already in the industry, having an MBA can help you stand out—not only from other people applying for jobs, but also among coworkers vying for promotions. Your MBA shows that you can balance philosophy with practice. It also proves your drive, focus, and passion. What boss wouldn’t love to see that in an employee?

Unique MBA Career Specializations

Graduating MBAs are no longer limited to jobs in traditional fields like investment banking, consulting, and financial services. In recent years, there have been increased opportunities for specializations in tech, entertainment, music, and health care. Here are a few real examples to get you thinking.

MBA for flight enthusiasts
The Aviation and Aviation Management MBA at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University (ERAU) can prepare you for various MBA careers in the

business of flight. Students concentrate on how modern business practices apply to aviation and aviation organizations.

ERAU keeps its curriculum current by obtaining input from aerospace representatives at organizations like NASA and the International Civil Aviation Organization.

MBA for sommeliers
Sonoma State University School of Business and Economics was the first to offer an MBA in the business practices of the wine industry. It’s located in California’s wine country, so it’s been able to winemakers.

Students can enroll in courses like Wine Industry Compliance and Law, International Supply Chain Management, and Marketing and Sales Strategies for Wine. One popular student club is the Wine Sense Club.

MBA for gamers
Students at the University of Nevada-Reno can choose nine elective credits from a variety of industry MBA specializations, including courses focused on the study of gambling and commercial gaming.

Is an MBA worth it?

Start to crunch these numbers by measuring your estimated business school investment against your projected future earnings to get an idea of the return on investment (ROI) on your MBA. Here are some tips for estimating your MBA ROI.

Do some addition: First, figure out some basics about the “I” in ROI, or your “investment” in business school. What are your target schools’

tuition and fees? Account for cost of living, travel, and related expenses, which can vary drastically from place to place.

Do some subtraction: Reduce the ‘I’ as much as possible. Explore scholarships and fellowships to offset the price, and apply early in Round One so you have time to be considered for these. See if your employer has a tuition assistance program, but remember that this may involve some kind of commitment to remain with the company for an agreed-upon amount of time.

Estimate: You can’t calculate your ROI without the ‘R’, so you might need to venture some educated guesses. How will your career change after graduating business school, and what is your expected earning increase? Look at industries in which graduates from your preferred MBA programs end up working and the average salaries of their positions. Make sure that the recruiters who target those schools are in fields that match your post-MBA goals.
